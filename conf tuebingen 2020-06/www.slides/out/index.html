Is there a fundamental question of action theory?

Why suppose it makes sense to ask outright which things are actions but not which things are red?

My sense is that the action terms (‘action‘, ’agent’, ’intention’ and the rest) as used by philosophers to pose a ‘fundamental question’ are not treated as technical terms but parts of ordinary language. But if Clark is right about terms being used contrastively (to put it roughly), the question as standardly posed cannot be asked at all.
You might think, in the case of red, that we should focus on the psychological capacity. The question about which things are red is trivial only because the psychological capacity is so shallow (mere categorization). But it is hard to interpret philosophers of action as doing the same, because psychological capacities to track agents and their actions are so much less well understood.

objection 2 : model vs construal

Source: Godfrey-Smith

e.g. model of a house

Model of a house. Initially it’s aspirational [construal 1]. They you win the lottery. Same model can be construed as a plan [construal 2]. Later, when the house is built, you use it as a map of the house [construal 3]. Then you realise that people are quite similar and that what you thought was a unique design is something that lots of other people would also want, so you use the model to predict what others will want [construal 4].

Models, in my sense, do not themselves say anything about the world.

Models are structures that can be used by scientists to say various different things about the world,

by means of commentaries that accompany models but are distinct from them’

\citep[p.~4]{godfrey-smith:2005_folk}.

Is there a fundamental question of action theory?

Suppose we reject the view that humans have a capacity to track agents and their actions which (i) involves a single model, and (ii) comes with construals that are widely-shared among humans.

Can we still as the ‘fundamental question’ of action theory?
