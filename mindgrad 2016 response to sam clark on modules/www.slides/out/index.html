Modularity
& Disunity:
Reply to
Sam Clarke

s.butterfill@warwick.ac.uk

superficial features

encapsulation

domain specificity

limited access

...

Encapsulation, domain specificity, limited access, and the rest are merely indicators of modularity.
What matters is not the absolute degree of encapsulation (say), but rather that fact that differences in degree of encapsulation (say) indicate differences in the nature of the underlying processes and representational formats.
This is because modularity is something that explains these properties rather than something that merely consists in them.

real essence

rule-govered computational process

distinctive representational format

As Sam writes in the paper, ‘Concerning how Fodorians should understand the natural kindness of modular input systems, my suggestion is just that modular systems might be distinguished in the human mind by an underlying type of computational process that governs their operations: i.e. that the essence of the modular kind might be a certain type of computational processing that is found in some, and only some, cognitive systems—the modular ones—and that entails and explains the manifestation of the superficial properties that these systems are seen to display to a striking degree.’
A threat that Sam is aware of: opponents of modularity will retort that Sam has raised the bar for objections so high that nothing could show there are no modules!
Sam’s retort (in the paper) is to emphasise that his view makes some predictions ...

‘predictions ... whenever we find a module we will find that the content it processes is like that processed by other modules from the perspective of the systems and unlike that processed by non-modules’

mindcater.com/...

It’s a familiar idea that things---sounds, say---can be specified using different representational formats.
Differences in format matter because they affect performance: some things that are difficult or impossible in one format (changing pitch, say) can be possible and perhaps even easy in another representational format.

Non-modular processes: multiple representational formats.

For example, in producing music (I suppose).
This is the beginnings of an objection to something Sam writes: ‘critics of such a view seem required to hold the opposite: they must hold that the form and structure of the representations will remain constant across these systems, at least from the point of view of the systems doing the computing.’

Modules: one representational format or many?

One the one hand, the claim that modularity has an essence suggests that it has to be one thing, and in turn might be taken to imply that there must be one kind of rule-goverened computational process and one kind of representational format common to all modules.
Sam himself appears to accept this view: ‘the essence of the modular kind might be a certain type of computational processing that is found in ... only ... the modular ... and that entails and explains the manifestation of the superficial properties that these systems are seen to display to a striking degree.’

vision -- navigation -- speech

On the other hand, it seems quite plausible that different modules will involve different representational formats. Consider the modules that Sam mentions: vision, speech and navigation.
Vision presumably deals with the arrangement of surfaces and objects in space, whereas navigation is about landmarks and speech concerns acoustic and bodily events.
Can we imagine that these all involve a common representational format? Suppose they don’t, and suppose, further, that the underlying processes differ significantly in kind. This may well mean, of course, that these things---speech perception, vision and navigation---evince charcateristics of modularity such as encapsulation and limited accessibilities to varying degrees to which.
Sam also writes that ‘critics must convincingly show that different modules manifest their distinctive properties in quite different ways such that these differences must be explained by the underlying properties or mechanisms of the systems themselves’

Maybe there is no one such thing as modularity ... but many?

I think this is not strictly consistent with the view Sam endorses in the paper, but I do think it’s the view that his talk pushes us towards. And it may be true.
After all, once we have the distinction between format and kind of process as a theoretical tool, why do we need there to be a single essence which all modules share?
